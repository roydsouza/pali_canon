## Overview

Chapter VIII of the Visuddhimagga is the most complete technical treatment of breath meditation in the classical Theravāda literature. Buddhaghosa works methodically through each of the sixteen steps taught in MN 118, giving precise definitions of the Pali terms, detailed practical instructions, and a full account of how the practice leads from the initial settling of the mind through the four jhānas to the fulfilment of the insight knowledges.

The chapter covers four major stages of practice:

| Stage | Vsm sections | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Counting** (*gaṇanā*) | §§ 145–187 | Stabilising attention on the breath by counting 1–10 |
| **Following** (*anubandhanā*) | §§ 188–203 | Dropping the count, attending to the whole breath continuously |
| **Contact** (*phusanā*) | §§ 204–210 | Settling at the touch-point; the arising of the *nimitta* |
| **Fixing** (*ṭhapanā*) | §§ 211–230 | Entering concentration through the *nimitta* to the jhānas |

## §§ 145–187 — The Six Preliminary Methods and Counting

### What "body" means in the first tetrad

Buddhaghosa opens with a question that is fundamental to the whole practice: when the first tetrad of ānāpānasati speaks of contemplating the body (*kāya*), which body is meant? His answer is unambiguous: in the context of breath meditation, *kāya* refers to the **breath-body** — the in-and-out breath experienced as a continuous flowing phenomenon — not the gross anatomical body.

This distinction matters for practice. You are not attending to the torso or the nostrils or the belly as objects in their own right. You are attending to the breath itself as a unified process: its length, its texture, its arising and passing.

### The six faults to avoid when beginning

Before counting, Buddhaghosa identifies six ways of incorrectly taking up the breath as an object: (1) counting too fast, (2) counting too slowly, (3) losing count, (4) attending to the interval between breaths rather than the breath, (5) attending to the body-parts through which the breath passes, (6) taking sounds as the object.

### Counting: one to ten, not zero, not eleven

The counting method: on each out-breath (or in-breath, depending on the teacher), count internally — one, two, three ... up to ten. Begin again at one. Do not count below one; do not count above ten. The purpose of counting is not arithmetic: it is to prevent the mind from taking any other object. The counting itself is dropped once the mind has stabilised enough to follow the breath without it.

Buddhaghosa distinguishes counting as one of six preliminary practices (*pubba-bhāgā*) for establishing the mind on the breath — it is a scaffold, not the building.

## §§ 188–203 — Following (*Anubandhanā*)

Once the mind can stay with the breath through ten cycles without counting, following begins: the meditator tracks the breath continuously without the aid of numbers, attending to it as it enters, as it fills the body, and as it exits. The key instruction is to stay at the touch-point — the point where the breath makes contact with the body — rather than following the breath inside the body. Buddhaghosa is explicit: "Do not follow the breath in; do not follow it out."

The touch-point differs by person: for some it is the nostrils; for others the upper lip; for still others a broader area. The point is consistency — once found, stay with it.

## §§ 204–215 — Contact, the Sign, and the Path Forward

### The arising of the *nimitta* (meditation sign)

As following practice deepens, a **nimitta** (meditation sign, *paṭibhāga-nimitta*) arises. This is not a visual image superimposed on the experience from outside; it is a naturally arising appearance of the concentrated mind's own luminosity. Different meditators describe it differently — as a patch of light, as a star, as a full moon, as a cotton ball — but these descriptions reflect individual differences in perception, not different objects.

Buddhaghosa is clear: the nimitta is not the breath itself. It is the **counterpart sign** (*paṭibhāga-nimitta*) — the appearance of the breath to a mind whose concentration has reached the neighbourhood concentration (*upacāra-samādhi*) threshold.

### From neighbourhood concentration to absorption

When the nimitta arises stably, the meditator should move attention from the breath to the nimitta, and from the nimitta into absorption (*appanā-samādhi*). The instruction is: do not examine the nimitta analytically; do not even look at it as an object in the ordinary sense. Rather, *enter into it* — let the mind's attention become suffused with it.

## §§ 216–230 — The Four Jhānas Through Breath Meditation

Buddhaghosa here applies the standard jhāna analysis to breath meditation specifically. The breath-nimitta as the meditation object supports all four jhānas; the progression through them follows the standard schema also found in the canonical suttas.

### First jhāna (*paṭhama-jhāna*)

**Five factors** are present:
- **Vitakka** (applied thought): the mind places itself on the nimitta
- **Vicāra** (sustained thought): the mind remains continuously on the nimitta
- **Pīti** (rapture/joy): a pervasive sense of well-being that arises with the abandonment of the hindrances
- **Sukha** (pleasure/happiness): the pleasurable quality of the concentrated state
- **Ekaggatā** (one-pointedness): unification of the mind on the nimitta

**Five hindrances abandoned**: sensual desire (*kāmacchanda*), ill will (*vyāpāda*), sloth-and-torpor (*thīna-middha*), restlessness-and-worry (*uddhacca-kukkucca*), and doubt (*vicikicchā*). The jhāna factors directly oppose the hindrances: *vitakka* counters sloth-and-torpor; *vicāra* counters doubt; *pīti* counters ill will; *sukha* counters restlessness; *ekaggatā* counters sensual desire.

> [!QUOTE]- Bhikkhu Bodhi — description of the first jhāna (§§ 216–222)
> *[Paste relevant passage from your copy of The Path of Purification here]*

### Second jhāna (*dutiya-jhāna*)

*Vitakka* and *vicāra* are abandoned. The mind enters the nimitta without the scaffolding of applied and sustained thought — it is already suffused with the object. **Three factors remain**: pīti, sukha, ekaggatā. A new quality arises: **sampasādana** (internal confidence/clarity), described as the mind settling into its own nature without the coarser activity of thinking.

### Third jhāna (*tatiya-jhāna*)

*Pīti* fades — not forcibly suppressed, but naturally transcended as the concentration deepens. What remains is **sukha** and **ekaggatā**, accompanied by **upekkhā** (equanimity). The third jhāna is described by the Buddha himself as the state where "one meditates with equanimity, mindful and fully alert" (*upekhako ca viharati*). Buddhaghosa notes that the practitioners of the third jhāna are praised by the noble ones for their equanimity.

### Fourth jhāna (*catuttha-jhāna*)

Both *sukha* and *dukkha* are transcended — even the subtle pleasure of the third jhāna is released. The mind rests in pure **upekkhā** and **ekaggatā**. The breath becomes so refined in the fourth jhāna that it may appear to stop entirely. This is not actually cessation of breathing but its becoming imperceptible to normal awareness; the fourth jhāna is the basis for the development of the supernormal powers (Ch. XII) and the formless attainments (Ch. X).

> [!QUOTE]- Bhikkhu Bodhi — on the second through fourth jhānas (§§ 222–230)

## Key Pali terms in this chapter

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **ānāpānasati** | Mindfulness of in-and-out breathing (*ānāpāna* = in-and-out breath; *sati* = mindfulness) |
| **gaṇanā** | Counting — the first of the six preliminary practices |
| **anubandhanā** | Following — continuous tracking of the breath without counting |
| **phusanā** | Contact/touching — settling at the touch-point |
| **ṭhapanā** | Fixing/establishing — entering absorption through the nimitta |
| **nimitta** | Meditation sign; here, the *paṭibhāga-nimitta* (counterpart sign) that arises in neighbourhood concentration |
| **upacāra-samādhi** | Neighbourhood concentration — the threshold of access, just before absorption |
| **appanā-samādhi** | Absorption concentration — full jhāna |
| **pīti** | Rapture; the quality of joy/well-being associated with the first and second jhānas |
| **sukha** | Pleasure/happiness; subtler than pīti; persists into the third jhāna |
| **upekkhā** | Equanimity; the dominant quality of the fourth jhāna |
| **ekaggatā** | One-pointedness; unification of the mind; present in all four jhānas |
| **vitakka** | Applied thought; the mind's initial placement on the object |
| **vicāra** | Sustained thought; the mind's continued engagement with the object |
