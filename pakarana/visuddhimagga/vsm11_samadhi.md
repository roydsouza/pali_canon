## Overview

Chapter XI is where Buddhaghosa steps back from the specific meditation subjects (treated in Ch. IV–X) and gives a systematic, philosophically precise account of concentration (*samādhi*) itself. He defines concentration at multiple levels, analyses the jhāna factors with Abhidhamma precision, describes the phenomenology of each jhāna, and explains the five masteries over the jhānas that a meditator must develop before the deep samādhi can serve as a basis for insight.

This chapter is the technical reference for the jhāna system; Chapter VIII (ānāpānasati) is the practical instruction. Read them together.

---

## Part 1 — What Is Samādhi?

### Three grades of concentration

Buddhaghosa distinguishes three levels of concentration that are relevant for practice:

| Level | Pali term | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Momentary | *Khaṇika-samādhi* | The moment-to-moment focus of mind on an object; present in all mental activity; the basis for bare insight practice (*sukkha-vipassanā*) |
| Neighbourhood | *Upacāra-samādhi* | Access concentration; the threshold of absorption, stabilised enough to use as the basis for vipassanā or for entering jhāna |
| Absorption | *Appanā-samādhi* | Full jhāna; the mind is fully absorbed in the object with the jhāna factors at full strength |

The canonical suttas use *samādhi* to mean absorption concentration (*appanā*). Most meditators will work at the neighbourhood level for extended periods before absorption arises.

## Part 2 — The Five Jhāna Factors

Buddhaghosa analyses the first jhāna as having five **jhāna factors** (*jhānaṅga*), which are not additions to the ordinary mind but qualities that arise when the hindrances are suspended and the mind unifies around its object.

| Factor | Pali | Function | Opposes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Applied thought | *Vitakka* | Places the mind on the object | Sloth-and-torpor |
| Sustained thought | *Vicāra* | Keeps the mind continuously on the object | Doubt |
| Rapture | *Pīti* | Pervades the mind with joy | Ill will |
| Pleasure | *Sukha* | The pleasurable quality of the concentrated state | Restlessness-and-worry |
| One-pointedness | *Ekaggatā* | Unification of mind | Sensual desire |

The five factors directly oppose the five hindrances. This mapping is not incidental — it explains the phenomenology of entering concentration: as each hindrance subsides, its opposing jhāna factor strengthens. The two processes are the same event described from different directions.

> [!QUOTE]- Bhikkhu Bodhi — on the jhāna factors opposing the hindrances (§§ 11–25)
> *[Paste relevant passage from your copy of The Path of Purification here]*

## Part 3 — The Four Jhānas

### First jhāna (*paṭhama-jhāna*)

**Factors**: vitakka, vicāra, pīti, sukha, ekaggatā (five factors).

The first jhāna is characterised by **seclusion** (*viveka*) — not physical seclusion but the mind's withdrawal from the five hindrances. The canonical simile (DN 2): a skilled bathman kneads soapstone powder with water until the moisture pervades the ball without excess water dripping — so the meditator suffuses the body with pīti and sukha without remainder.

The activity of vitakka and vicāra gives the first jhāna a quality that Buddhaghosa calls *savitakkasavicāra* — "with applied and sustained thought." This is not the gross discursive thinking of the hindrances; it is a refined mental application that is still present, the last trace of intentional placement. Many meditators experience the first jhāna as having a kind of texture or vibrancy — this is the vitakka/vicāra activity at its most refined.

> [!QUOTE]- Bhikkhu Bodhi — on the first jhāna (§§ 32–50)
> *[Paste relevant passage from your copy of The Path of Purification here]*

### Second jhāna (*dutiya-jhāna*)

**Factors**: pīti, sukha, ekaggatā (three factors). Vitakka and vicāra abandoned.

The canonical simile (DN 2): a lake fed by an underground spring — the cool water suffuses the lake from within, with no external source. So pīti and sukha suffuse the body of the second-jhāna meditator, arising from within the concentration itself rather than from any intentional effort.

The second jhāna is characterised by **internal confidence** (*sampasāda*) and the absence of applied and sustained thought. The mind has found its own footing in the object; it no longer needs to be placed there. This transition is often experienced as a significant quieting — a falling-away of the last trace of effort.

> [!QUOTE]- Bhikkhu Bodhi — on the second jhāna (§§ 51–70)
> *[Paste relevant passage from your copy of The Path of Purification here]*

### Third jhāna (*tatiya-jhāna*)

**Factors**: sukha, ekaggatā (two factors). Pīti abandoned.

The canonical simile (DN 2): lotus flowers growing in a lake, submerged in cool water, suffused with it from root to tip. So the third-jhāna meditator's body is suffused with sukha without pīti.

Pīti fades not through effort but through the natural refinement of concentration. The quality of pīti — which involves physical manifestations like goose-bumps, tingling, lightness, or waves of pleasure — is coarser than pure sukha. As concentration deepens, the coarser quality drops away, leaving a finer, more even happiness. The third jhāna is accompanied by **equanimity** (*upekkhā*) and **mindfulness** (*sati*) — the noble ones praise those who abide here as "one with equanimity, mindful."

> [!QUOTE]- Bhikkhu Bodhi — on the third jhāna (§§ 71–90)
> *[Paste relevant passage from your copy of The Path of Purification here]*

### Fourth jhāna (*catuttha-jhāna*)

**Factors**: upekkhā, ekaggatā (two factors, but now upekkhā replaces sukha). Both sukha and dukkha abandoned.

The canonical simile (DN 2): a person sitting covered with a white cloth, the cloth touching every part of the body — so equanimity pervades the meditator's body completely.

The fourth jhāna is described as having **purity of equanimity and mindfulness** (*upekkhāsatipārisuddhi*). Even the subtle pleasure of the third jhāna has been transcended. The mind rests in complete equanimity — neither drawn toward pleasurable experience nor pushing away unpleasant experience. This equanimity is not indifference; it is the mind's natural state when completely freed from grasping.

Key characteristics:
- The breath becomes imperceptible — not stopped, but so refined as to be undetectable by ordinary means
- The mind is like a polished mirror: clear, receptive, undisturbed
- This state is the **basis for the supernormal powers** (Ch. XII) and the **basis for insight** (Parts XIV–XXII)

> [!QUOTE]- Bhikkhu Bodhi — on the fourth jhāna (§§ 91–111)
> *[Paste relevant passage from your copy of The Path of Purification here]*

## Part 4 — The Five Masteries (*Vasī*)

Buddhaghosa insists that attaining jhāna is not enough — the meditator must develop **mastery** (*vasī*) over each jhāna before it can serve as a stable basis for the remaining path. The five masteries:

1. **Adverting mastery** (*āvajjana-vasī*): ability to advert to a jhāna at will after emerging from it
2. **Attaining mastery** (*samāpajjana-vasī*): ability to enter a jhāna quickly
3. **Resolving mastery** (*adhiṭṭhāna-vasī*): ability to remain in jhāna for a pre-determined duration
4. **Emerging mastery** (*vuṭṭhāna-vasī*): ability to emerge from jhāna at a pre-determined time without disturbance
5. **Reviewing mastery** (*paccavekkhaṇa-vasī*): ability to review the jhāna factors, their presence, and their characteristics

Buddhaghosa's instruction: the meditator should practise entering and emerging, entering and emerging, until the jhāna is as familiar and reliable as walking into a room. Only then should the meditator use the jhāna as the basis for insight.

> [!QUOTE]- Bhikkhu Bodhi — on the five masteries (§§ 120–134)

## The jhānas and the canonical texts

The five-factor analysis of the first jhāna (vitakka, vicāra, pīti, sukha, ekaggatā) is Buddhaghosa's Abhidhamma-based systematisation. The canonical suttas describe the jhānas experientially:

> *"Quite withdrawn from sensual pleasures, withdrawn from unskillful qualities, they enter and remain in the first jhāna, which has the rapture and pleasure born of seclusion, while placing the mind and keeping it connected."* — [[dn2|DN 2]]

The Vsm chapter should always be read **alongside** the canonical formulas, not instead of them. The canonical description conveys the phenomenology; the Vsm analysis provides the technical precision.
