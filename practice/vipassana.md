# Vipassanā (Insight Meditation)

**Navigation**: [[../INDEX|Pali Canon Vault Index]]

---

## Description

**Vipassanā** (Insight) is the practice of developing direct, experiential understanding of the nature of reality. It is traditionally paired with *samatha* (tranquility) as two qualities of mind developed in tandem to achieve liberation. While *samatha* calms and stabilizes the mind, *vipassanā* uses that stability to investigate and see through the delusions that bind the mind to suffering.

The core of Vipassanā practice is the contemplation of all conditioned phenomena in terms of the **Three Marks of Existence** (*tilakkhaṇa*):
1. **Impermanence** (*anicca*): Discerning that all physical and mental phenomena arise and pass away, never remaining the same for two consecutive moments.
2. **Suffering** (*dukkha*): Recognizing that because conditioned things are impermanent, they are inherently unstable, unsatisfactory, and incapable of providing lasting happiness.
3. **Non-self** (*attā* / *anattā*): Discerning that no conditioned or unconditioned phenomenon contains a permanent, independent essence, soul, or controller.

In practice, this insight is developed by turning attention toward the **Five Aggregates** (*pañcakkhandhā*) or the **Six Sense Bases** (*saḷāyatana*). By observing contact, feeling, perception, and intentions as they occur at the sense doors, the meditator sees that these are empty, causal processes, thereby cutting off craving and clinging.

## Sutta References

The following suttas and sections in the vault describe, analyze, or detail the practice of Vipassanā:

*   [[mn148|MN 148: Chachakkasutta]] — Provides a detailed guide to examining the six sense doors, showing how identification and attachment arise, and how non-identification leads to liberation.
*   [[mn28|MN 28: Mahāhatthipadopamasutta]] — Uses the simile of the elephant's footprint to detail the contemplation of the four physical elements and their impermanence.
*   [[mn140|MN 140: Dhātuvibhaṅgasutta]] — Instructs in the analysis of the six elements (earth, water, fire, wind, space, consciousness) and the stilled mind.
*   [[mn2|MN 2: Sabbāsavasutta]] — Outlines the seven methods to abandon the taints (*āsavā*), including the role of wise attention (*yoniso manasikāra*).
*   [[sn36|SN 36.6: Sallasutta / SN 36.11: Rahogatasutta]] — Examines how to observe feelings (*vedanā*) and their underlying tendencies to greed, aversion, and ignorance.
*   [[mn10|MN 10: Satipaṭṭhānasutta]] (and [[dn22|DN 22: Mahāsatipaṭṭhānasutta]]) — The foundational manuals for developing mindfulness and insight across the four domains of body, feelings, mind, and principles.
