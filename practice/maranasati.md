# Maraṇasati (Mindfulness of Death)

**Navigation**: [[../INDEX|Pali Canon Vault Index]]

---

## Description

**Maraṇasati** (Mindfulness or recollection of death) is a practice designed to cultivate spiritual urgency (*saṃvega*), destroy complacency, and sever attachment to worldly gains, relationships, and bodily vanity. The Buddha frequently exhorted practitioners to meditate on the certainty of death and the uncertainty of when it will arrive, declaring that mindfulness of death, when developed and cultivated, is of great fruit and benefit, culminating in the deathless (*amata*).

The practice of Maraṇasati consists of reflecting on the following themes:
1. **Inevitability**: Life is unstable, and all beings—kings, sages, and ordinary people—must eventually decay and pass away.
2. **Fragility**: The human body is fragile, exposed to numerous external dangers (disease, wild animals, accidents) and internal imbalances (elements, humors) that can end life in an instant.
3. **Uncertainty**: We do not know when we will die, where we will die, under what circumstances, or where we will be reborn.
4. **Urgency**: Realizing that death could happen at any moment (even within the space of a single breath or chew of food), the meditator is spurred to practice diligently *right now* to root out the defilements before it is too late.

In the suttas, a monk practicing Maraṇasati reflects at dawn and dusk: "If I live only for the duration of a single breath, or the time it takes to chew a mouthful of food, and I keep my mind focused on the Buddha's teachings, that is diligent practice." It is the ultimate tool for overcoming laziness, procrastination, and greed.

## Sutta References

The following suttas and sections in the vault describe, analyze, or detail the practice of Maraṇasati:

*   [[an6_19|AN 6.19: Maraṇassatisutta (1st)]] — The Buddha asks his monks how often they practice mindfulness of death. He rebukes those who practice it daily or hourly, declaring that only those who practice it with the urgency of a single breath are truly diligent (*appamatta*).
*   [[an6_20|AN 6.20: Maraṇassatisutta (2nd)]] — Details the practical reflection of a monk at dawn and dusk, reviewing whether there are any unwholesome states left in the mind that would lead to a bad rebirth if they died that night.
*   [[an8_73|AN 8.73: Maraṇassatisutta]] — Encourages the frequent contemplation of mortality to overcome passion and laziness.
*   [[an10_60|AN 10.60: Girimānandasutta]] — Lists the contemplation of death (*maraṇasaññā*) as one of the ten contemplations that can cure illness.
