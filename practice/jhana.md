# Jhāna (Meditative Absorption)

**Navigation**: [[../INDEX|Pali Canon Vault Index]]

---

## Description

**Jhāna** is a state of deep mental unification, stillness, and absorption. In the Gradual Training (*anupubbasikkhā*), it is developed after the purification of ethical conduct (*sīla*), guarding the sense faculties, mindfulness and situational awareness, contentment, and the overcoming of the five hindrances (*nīvaraṇā*). 

There are four rupa-jhānas described throughout the suttas:
1. **First Jhāna**: Secluded from sensual pleasures and unwholesome states, one enters and remains in the first absorption, which is accompanied by directed thought and evaluation (*vitakka-vicāra*), and the rapture and pleasure (*pīti-sukha*) born of seclusion.
2. **Second Jhāna**: With the stilling of directed thought and evaluation, one enters and remains in the second absorption, which is accompanied by internal confidence and unification of mind without directed thought or evaluation, and the rapture and pleasure born of concentration (*samādhi-ja*).
3. **Third Jhāna**: With the fading away of rapture, one remains equanimous, mindful, and alert, experiencing physical pleasure (*sukha*) of which the noble ones say: "Equanimous and mindful, one dwells in pleasure," and so one enters and remains in the third absorption.
4. **Fourth Jhāna**: With the giving up of pleasure and pain, and the ending of former gladness and sadness, one enters and remains in the fourth absorption, which is neither painful nor pleasant, and features the purification of mindfulness through equanimity (*upekkhā-sati-parisuddhi*).

Practicing jhāna provides the mental stability, flexibility, and absolute clarity required to investigate the nature of reality and develop liberating insight (*vipassanā*).

## Sutta References

The following suttas and sections in the vault describe, analyze, or detail the practice of Jhāna:

*   [[dn2|DN 2: Sāmaññaphalasutta]] — Provides the classic similes for each of the four jhānas (the bathman, the lake with a spring, the lotuses in water, and the clean white cloth).
*   [[an5_28|AN 5.28: Pañcaṅgikasutta]] — Details the five factors of noble concentration (including the same four similes, plus a fifth factor of reviewing/monitoring).
*   [[an9_36|AN 9.36: Jhānasutta]] — Analyzes how each of the jhānas serves as a footing for the destruction of the taints (*āsavānaṃ khayāya*).
*   [[mn111|MN 111: Anupadasutta]] — Illustrates Sāriputta's factor-by-factor mindfulness during his jhāna practice, showing how he tracked mental states as they arose, stood, and passed away.
*   [[mn119|MN 119: Kāyagatāsatisutta]] — Lists the four jhānas as primary forms of mindfulness of the body.
