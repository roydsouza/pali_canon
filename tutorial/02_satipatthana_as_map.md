## The two versions

The vault contains two versions of the Satipaṭṭhāna teaching:

| Sutta | Length | What it adds |
|---|---|---|
| [[mn10|MN 10: Satipaṭṭhānasutta]] | ~5,700 words | The foundational version |
| [[dn22|DN 22: Mahāsatipaṭṭhānasutta]] | ~8,900 words | The expanded version — adds the Four Noble Truths as a fifth contemplation under the fourth foundation |

For daily practice, **MN 10 is the working text**. DN 22 is the deeper study text — use it when you want the doctrinal elaboration of the four truths within the satipaṭṭhāna framework.

Open MN 10 now in a split pane alongside this tutorial.

## The structure of the map

The refrain (*anupassanā*) is the heartbeat of the sutta. Every section ends with the same sequence:

> *"In this way, in regard to the body they meditate observing the body — internally, externally, and both internally and externally. They meditate observing the arising of phenomena in the body, the passing of phenomena in the body, and the arising and passing of phenomena in the body."*

This refrain appears **thirteen times** in MN 10 — once for each contemplation. Learning to hear it as a rhythmic marker is itself a practice. Each repetition signals: *you have completed one contemplation; now apply the same quality of attention to the next object.*

The four foundations, with their contemplations:

### Foundation 1 — Body (*kāyānupassanā*)
1. Breath (*ānāpāna*) — covered in Tutorial 1
2. Postures (*iriyāpatha*) — standing, sitting, walking, lying down
3. Clear comprehension (*sampajañña*) — knowing what you are doing while you do it
4. Reflection on the body's parts (*paṭikūlamanasikāra*) — the 31 body parts
5. The four elements (*dhātus*) — earth, water, fire, air
6. Nine charnel-ground contemplations (*sīvathikā*) — seeing the body's decay

### Foundation 2 — Feeling (*vedanānupassanā*)
Observing whether feeling-tone is pleasant, unpleasant, or neutral — and whether it is worldly or spiritual.

### Foundation 3 — Mind (*cittānupassanā*)
Sixteen mind-states observed directly: with/without lust, hatred, delusion; contracted, scattered, developed, concentrated, liberated.

### Foundation 4 — Phenomena (*dhammānupassanā*)
Five contemplations of mental and doctrinal objects:
1. The five hindrances
2. The five aggregates
3. The six sense bases
4. The seven awakening factors
5. The Four Noble Truths (expanded in DN 22)

## How to use this in practice

The map is not a sequential programme. You do not work through all thirteen contemplations in order each session. Instead:

**Daily practice:** Choose one contemplation as your primary object. Breath in Foundation 1 is the most common starting point. Body postures (Foundation 1, #2) is excellent for walking meditation. Feeling-tone (Foundation 2) is particularly powerful once breath practice is established.

**As a diagnostic tool:** When practice feels stuck or unclear, open MN 10 to Foundation 3 (*cittānupassanā*) and read the sixteen mind-states. Usually you can immediately recognize which state is present. Naming it precisely — not just "I feel restless" but "the mind is scattered; the mind is not concentrated" — is itself a practice move.

**Before sitting:** Read the refrain aloud once. Its rhythm sets the quality of attention before the session begins.

## The commentary on MN 10

Open [[mn10_att|MN 10 Commentary]] in a third pane or consult it serially. This is Buddhaghosa's *Papañcasūdanī* — 362 paragraphs, the most detailed canonical treatment of satipaṭṭhāna practice.

Three sections are especially valuable for practitioners:

1. **§1–§30** — the opening section defines *sati* (mindfulness) precisely and distinguishes it from mere memory or attention. Read this once to anchor your understanding of the term.
2. **The charnel-ground section** — Buddhaghosa's commentary on each of the nine stages of bodily decay is detailed and should be read slowly, not browsed. It is not pleasant reading; that is the point.
3. **The closing section on the awakening factors** — explains how sustained satipaṭṭhāna practice progressively develops the seven factors. This is the mechanism by which the practice produces liberation.

## Navigating between MN 10 and DN 22

The two suttas are identical through Foundation 3 and most of Foundation 4. DN 22 expands the final contemplation (the Four Noble Truths) with a full treatment of each truth, including the standard definition of the eight path factors under the fourth truth. If you are reading the Four Noble Truths section:

- Use **MN 10** for the compressed, meditative version
- Use **DN 22** for the doctrinally complete version — and follow the link to [[dn22_att|DN 22 Commentary]] for Buddhaghosa's expansion of the truths

Both suttas link back to the [[four_foundations_of_mindfulness]] mātikā, which lists every vault sutta connected to satipaṭṭhāna.
