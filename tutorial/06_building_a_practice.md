> **What you will be able to do after this tutorial:**
> Use the vault's reading paths and practice tools to structure a long-term programme — not just individual sessions, but a sustained practice arc across months and years.

---

## The difference between reading and practising

A vault of canonical texts can easily become something you read *about* rather than practice *with*. This is worth naming directly. The texts in this vault were spoken by someone who had crossed to the other shore; they are maps, not destinations. The risk of a well-equipped study environment is mistaking map quality for travel.

The reading paths are designed to prevent this. Each path is a sequence of texts organized around a **practice question**, not a doctrinal category. The question forces contact with the practice before the study.

## A suggested long-term structure

For someone coming to this vault primarily through meditation practice, a reasonable arc is:

**Months 1–3: Foundation**
- Sit daily with breath (MN 118 as reading support)
- Read through MN 10 once per week, one section at a time
- Use the Gradual Training path as the doctrinal frame for your whole practice

**Months 3–6: Deepening**
- Follow the Jhāna path once you have some stability of concentration
- Use the Vipassanā path to extend observation from breath to feeling to mind
- Open the MN 10 commentary when a specific passage raises a question

**Months 6–12: Broadening**
- Follow the Brahmavihāra path alongside the concentration practice
- Use the question-driven paths when specific situations arise in sitting
- Begin working with the mātikā web for doctrinal study between sessions

**Ongoing: Pali engagement**
- Start the graded reader sequence when you feel curiosity about the original language
- Add vocabulary to the memorization log as terms become personally significant
- Use the Pali-only toggle for recitation of suttas you know well in English

This arc is a suggestion, not a programme. The vault supports whatever sequence your practice actually takes.

## The practice notes and memorization log

Two `practice/` files support your ongoing work:

**[[practice/memorization_log|Memorization Log]]** — Track which verses and passages you are committing to memory. The format has fields for sutta reference, Pali text, English, date added, and review status. Verses worth memorizing early: the opening of Snp 1.8 (Metta Sutta), the refrain from MN 10/DN 22, and the sixteen-step formula from MN 118.

**[[practice/INDEX|Practice Dashboard]]** — An overview of the practice support materials: session notes template, memorization tracker, and links to protective chanting texts.

## Using the paritta texts

The [[paritta/INDEX|Paritta Index]] collects the canonical protective chanting texts in recitation order. For daily practice, the standard sequence is:

1. [[snp1_8|Snp 1.8 — Karaṇīya Mettasutta]] (Metta Sutta) — the most recited paritta in the Theravāda tradition
2. [[snp2_1|Snp 2.1 — Ratanasutta]] (Jewel Discourse)
3. [[snp2_4|Snp 2.4 — Maṅgalasutta]] (Discourse on Blessings)
4. [[an4_67|AN 4.67 — Ahirājasutta]] (Snake Discourse)

Each paritta has a Pali-only recitation version under `paritta/` — open it, press **Cmd+R** if viewing the mūla file, and use the Pali text directly. The recitation versions are compiled continuous Pali with no interleaved English, formatted for chanting.

## A closing note

The Dhamma Practice Workstation is a tool. Tools are useful in proportion to how much they are used, and useless in proportion to how much they are studied instead. The most sophisticated vault in the world does not substitute for one hour of sitting with the breath.

Use these texts to inform your practice. Use your practice to deepen your reading of these texts. Let the two reinforce each other, and neither will plateau.
