# Tutorial 1: Sitting with the Ānāpānasati Sutta

**Navigation**: [[INDEX|Vault Home]] / [[tutorial/INDEX|Tutorials]]

> [!NOTE]
> **What you will be able to do after this tutorial:**
> Open the Ānāpānasati Sutta, read it alongside the classical commentary, understand the structure of the sixteen steps, and use the sutta as a direct support for a sitting session.

---

## Step 1 — Open the sutta

Open [[mn118|MN 118: Ānāpānasatisutta]] in one pane. If you want to follow this tutorial at the same time, split the view: **Cmd+\\** opens a second pane, and you can drag this page into one side and MN 118 into the other.

What you are looking at is the **mūla** (root text) — the canonical Pali in bold, with Bhikkhu Sujato's English translation in italics immediately below each phrase. This interleaving keeps the Pali and its meaning in constant contact. You are never reading a translation divorced from its original.

---

## Step 2 — Understand the structure

The Ānāpānasati Sutta teaches **sixteen steps** of breath meditation, organized into four *tetrads* (groups of four). Each tetrad corresponds to one of the four foundations of mindfulness:

| Tetrad | Foundation | Steps |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | Body (*kāya*) | Steps 1–4: long breath, short breath, experiencing the whole body, calming bodily formations |
| 2nd | Feeling (*vedanā*) | Steps 5–8: experiencing rapture, pleasure, mental formations, calming mental formations |
| 3rd | Mind (*citta*) | Steps 9–12: experiencing the mind, gladdening, concentrating, liberating the mind |
| 4th | Phenomena (*dhammā*) | Steps 13–16: contemplating impermanence, dispassion, cessation, relinquishment |

The sutta ends by showing how ānāpānasati, practised and developed, **fulfils the four foundations of mindfulness**, which in turn fulfil the seven awakening factors, which in turn fulfil knowledge and liberation. This is not a technique — it is a complete path.

---

## Step 3 — Read slowly; use the toggle

Read the first tetrad (steps 1–4) at meditation pace. Do not rush to the translation. When you encounter a Pali term you want to understand in its original form, stay with it.

When you want to read **Pali only** — for recitation, or to test your comprehension — press **Cmd+R**. The English lines disappear. Press again to bring them back. This toggle is the most-used feature in the vault for practitioners who are developing their Pali.

---

## Step 4 — Look up a word

**Double-click any bold Pali word** to open the Simsapa DPD sidebar. Try it on:

- **ānāpānasati** — mindfulness of in-and-out breathing
- **kāya** — body
- **passaddhi** — tranquillity (appears in step 4 and step 8)
- **bojjhaṅga** — awakening factor (in the closing section)

The DPD shows the headword, grammatical form, root, and usage examples. You do not need to understand Pali grammar to use this — just let the definitions deepen your reading of the English.

## Step 6 — Read the commentary

Open [[mn118_att|MN 118 Commentary (Aṭṭhakathā)]] alongside the mūla. The commentary is Buddhaghosa's *Papañcasūdanī* in classical Pali with paragraph anchors (`§1`, `§2`, …). You do not need to read Pali to use it — but even if you only glance at the structure, you will notice that Buddhaghosa is systematic: he works through every phrase of the root text in sequence.

For practice purposes, the commentary is most useful for three things:
1. **Precise definition of technical terms** — Buddhaghosa defines exactly what *kāya* means in the context of the first tetrad (it is the breath-body, not the gross physical body)
2. **Practical instruction** — the commentary gives detailed meditation guidance that is implicit in the terse root text
3. **Doctrinal cross-references** — phrases like "fulfils the seven awakening factors" are unpacked with references to other canonical passages

> [!TIP]
> You do not need to read the commentary every time you sit. Think of it as a reference you consult when a passage in the mūla raises a question. Read the mūla as your primary sitting text; open the commentary when something needs clarification.

---

## Step 7 — Sit

Close the tutorials. Open MN 118 alone, full-screen, in Reading Mode. Press **Cmd+R** if you want Pali-only. Read through the sixteen steps once before you sit. Then sit.

The structure of the sixteen steps is the structure of the practice. You do not need to accomplish all sixteen in a single session. The first tetrad alone — settling on the breath, experiencing the whole body of the breath, calming the breath — is sufficient for a lifetime of practice.
