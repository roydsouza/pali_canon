### 8. Ānāpānassatisuttavaṇṇanā


### §144

**144. Evaṃ me sutanti ānāpānassatisuttaṃ.**  
*144. "Evaṁ me sutaṁ" (Thus have I heard) refers to the Ānāpānasati Sutta.*

**Tattha aññehi cāti ṭhapetvā pāḷiyaṃ āgate dasa there aññehipi abhiññātehi bahūhi sāvakehi saddhiṃ.**  
*There, "aññehi ca" (and with others) means, setting aside the ten senior elders mentioned in the Pali text, together with many other well-known senior disciples.*

**Tadā kira mahā bhikkhusaṅgho ahosi aparicchinnagaṇano.**  
*At that time, it is said, the great community of monks was of unmeasurable number.*

**Ovadanti anusāsantīti āmisasaṅgahena dhammasaṅgahena cāti dvīhi saṅgahehi saṅgaṇhitvā kammaṭṭhānovādānusāsanīhi ovadanti ca anusāsanti ca.**  
*"Ovadanti anusāsanti" (advising and instructing) means helping them with the two kinds of help—material help and Dhamma help—and advising and instructing them with instructions and advice on meditation subjects (kammaṭṭhāna).*

**Te cāti cakāro āgamasandhimattaṃ.**  
*In "te ca" (and they), the letter "ca" is a grammatical conjunction insert.*

**Uḷāraṃ pubbenāparaṃ visesaṃ jānantīti sīlaparipūraṇādito pubbavisesato uḷārataraṃ aparaṃ kasiṇaparikammādivisesaṃ jānantīti attho.**  
*"Uḷāraṃ pubbenāparaṃ visesaṃ jānanti" (realized a higher distinction than they had before) means they realized a higher subsequent distinction, such as the preparation for kasina meditation, which is superior to the initial distinction consisting of the purification of virtue.*

### §145

**145. Āraddhoti tuṭṭho.**  
*145. "Āraddho" (satisfied) means pleased.*

**Appattassa pattiyāti appattassa arahattassa pāpuṇanatthaṃ.**  
*"Appattassa pattiyā" (for attaining the unattained) means for the purpose of reaching the unreached state of Arahantship.*

**Sesapadadvayepi ayameva attho.**  
*The same meaning applies to the next two phrases ("achieving the unachieved" and "realizing the unrealized").*

**Komudiṃ cātumāsininti pacchimakattikacātumāsapuṇṇamaṃ.**  
*"Komudiṁ cātumāsiniṁ" refers to the full moon of the fourth month (Kattika), the last month of the rainy season.*

**Sā hi kumudānaṃ atthitāya komudī, catunnaṃ vassikānaṃ māsānaṃ pariyosānattā cātumāsinīti vuccati.**  
*It is called komudī because the white water-lilies (kumuda) bloom at that time; it is called cātumāsinī because it is the completion of the four rainy months.*

**Āgamessāmīti udikkhissāmi, ajja apavāretvā yāva sā āgacchati, tāva katthaci agantvā idheva vasissāmīti attho.**  
*"Āgamessāmi" (I will wait) means "I will look forward to it; having completed the invitation (pavāraṇā) today, I will remain here without going elsewhere until that date comes."*

**Iti bhikkhūnaṃ pavāraṇasaṅgahaṃ anujānanto evamāha.**  
*Thus he spoke, permitting the monks to remain for the invitation period.*

**Pavāraṇasaṅgaho nāma ñattidutiyena kammena diyyati kassa panesa diyyati, kassa na diyyatīti.**  
*"Pavāraṇasaṅgaho" (the postponement of the invitation) is given by a formal action with a single motion (ñattidutiya kamma). To whom is it given, and to whom is it not given?*

**Akārakassa tāva bālaputhujjanassa na diyyati, tathā āraddhavipassakassa ceva ariyasāvakassa ca.**  
*First of all, it is not given to an inactive, foolish worldling (bālaputhujjana), nor to an active meditator of insight (āraddhavipassaka) or a noble disciple (ariyasāvaka).*

**Yassa pana samatho vā taruṇo hoti vipassanā vā, tassa diyyati.**  
*But it is given to one whose calm (samatha) or insight (vipassanā) is still immature.*

**Bhagavāpi tadā bhikkhūnaṃ cittācāraṃ parivīmaṃsanto samathavipassanānaṃ taruṇabhāvaṃ ñatvā – ‘‘mayi ajja pavārente disāsu vassaṃvuṭṭhā bhikkhū idha osarissanti.**  
*The Blessed One, examining the state of mind of the monks at that time and knowing the immaturity of their calm and insight, thought: "If I hold the invitation today, the monks who spent the rains in different directions will assemble here.*

**Tato ime bhikkhū vuḍḍhatarehi bhikkhūhi senāsane gahite visesaṃ nibbattetuṃ na sakkhissanti.**  
*Then these monks, when their lodgings are taken by the more senior monks, will not be able to produce any distinction.*

**Sacepi cārikaṃ pakkamissāmi, imesaṃ vasanaṭṭhānaṃ dullabhameva bhavissati.**  
*Even if I go on a walking tour, finding a dwelling place for them will be very difficult.*

**Mayi pana apavārente bhikkhūpi imaṃ sāvatthiṃ na osarissanti, ahampi cārikaṃ na pakkamissāmi, evaṃ imesaṃ bhikkhūnaṃ vasanaṭṭhānaṃ apalibuddhaṃ bhavissati.**  
*But if I postpone the invitation, the monks will not assemble in Sāvatthī, and I will not go on a tour either; thus, the dwelling places of these monks will remain free of obstacles.*

**Te attano attano vasanaṭṭhāne phāsu viharantā samathavipassanā thāmajātā katvā visesaṃ nibbattetuṃ sakkhissantī’’ti so taṃdivasaṃ apavāretvā kattikapuṇṇamāyaṃ pavāressāmīti bhikkhūnaṃ pavāraṇasaṅgahaṃ anujāni.**  
*They, living comfortably in their respective dwellings, making their calm and insight strong, will be able to produce distinction." Thinking thus, he did not hold the invitation on that day, but allowed the postponement of the invitation for the monks, declaring: "I will hold the invitation on the full moon of Kattika."*

**Pavāraṇasaṅgahasmiñhi laddhe yassa nissayapaṭipannassa ācariyupajjhāyā pakkamanti, sopi ‘‘sace patirūpo nissayadāyako āgamissati, tassa santike nissayaṃ gaṇhissāmī’’ti yāva gimhānaṃ pacchimamāsā vasituṃ labhati.**  
*Indeed, when the postponement is granted, if the teacher or preceptor of a monk who is under dependence leaves, that monk can still think: "If a suitable dependence-giver arrives, I will take dependence under him," and is allowed to stay until the last month of the hot season.*

**Sacepi saṭṭhivassā bhikkhū āgacchanti, tassa senāsanaṃ gahetuṃ na labhanti.**  
*Even if monks of sixty years' standing arrive, they cannot take his lodging.*

**Ayañca pana pavāraṇasaṅgaho ekassa dinnopi sabbesaṃ dinnoyeva hoti.**  
*Furthermore, this postponement of the invitation, even if given to one, is given to all.*

**Sāvatthiṃ osarantīti bhagavatā pavāraṇasaṅgaho dinnoti sutasutaṭṭhāneyeva yathāsabhāvena ekaṃ māsaṃ vasitvā kattikapuṇṇamāya uposathaṃ katvā osarante sandhāya idaṃ vuttaṃ.**  
*"Sāvatthiṃ osaranti" (came down to Sāvatthī) is said concerning those who, hearing that the Blessed One had granted the postponement of the invitation, stayed for one month in their respective places, and then assembled on the full moon of Kattika after performing the Uposatha.*

**Pubbenāparanti idha taruṇasamathavipassanāsu kammaṃ katvā samathavipassanā thāmajātā akaṃsu, ayaṃ pubbe viseso nāma.**  
*"Pubbenāparaṃ" (former and subsequent) means: here, having worked on immature calm and insight, they made their calm and insight strong—this is the "former distinction."*

**Tato samāhitena cittena saṅkhāre sammasitvā keci sotāpattiphalaṃ…pe… keci arahattaṃ sacchikariṃsu. Ayaṃ aparo uḷāro viseso nāma.**  
*Then, with concentrated minds, contemplating formations, some realized the fruit of stream-entry... pe ... some realized Arahantship—this is the "subsequent higher distinction."*

### §146

**146. Alanti yuttaṃ.**  
*146. "Alaṃ" means fitting or worthy.*

**Yojanagaṇanānīti ekaṃ yojanaṃ yojanameva, dasapi yojanāni yojanāneva, tato uddhaṃ yojanagaṇanānīti vuccanti.**  
*"Yojanagaṇanāni" (many leagues): one league (yojana) is just a league, ten leagues are just leagues, but beyond that, they are called "many leagues" (yojanagaṇanāni).*

**Idha pana yojanasatampi yojanasahassampiadhippetaṃ.**  
*Here, however, a hundred leagues or a thousand leagues are meant.*

**Puṭosenāpīti puṭosaṃ vaiccati pātheyyaṃ. taṃ pātheyyaṃ gahetvāpi upasaṅkamituṃ yuttamevāti attho.**  
*"Puṭosenāpi" (even carrying provisions in a knapsack): puṭosa means provisions for a journey. The meaning is: it is indeed worthy to approach even carrying provisions for the journey.*

**‘‘Puṭaṃsenā’’tipi pāṭho, tassattho – puṭo aṃse assāti puṭaṃso, tena puṭaṃsena, aṃse pātheyyapuṭaṃ vahantenāpīti vuttaṃ hoti.**  
*There is also the reading puṭaṃsena, meaning: one who has a bag on his shoulder is puṭaṃsa; by that puṭaṃsa, it is meant "even carrying the provision-bag on one's shoulder."*

### §147

**147. Idāni evarūpehi caraṇehi samannāgatā ettha bhikkhū atthīti dassetuṃ santi, bhikhāvetiādimāha.**  
*147. Now, to show that there are monks here endowed with such conduct, he said "santi, bhikkhave" (there are, monks...) and so forth.*

**Tattha catunnaṃ satipaṭṭhānānantiādīni tesaṃ bhikkhūnaṃ abhiniviṭṭhakammaṭṭhānadassanatthaṃ vuttāni.**  
*There, "catunnaṃ satipaṭṭhānānaṃ" (of the four foundations of mindfulness) etc. are spoken to show the meditation subjects that those monks had undertaken.*

**Tattha sattatiṃsa bodhipakkhiyadhammā lokiyalokuttarā kathitā.**  
*Here, the thirty-seven aids to awakening (bodhipakkhiyadhammā) are spoken of as both mundane and supramundane.*

**Tatra hi ye bhikkhū tasmiṃ khaṇe maggaṃ bhāventi, tesaṃ lokuttarā honti.**  
*For those monks who are developing the path at that moment, they are supramundane;*

**Āraddhavipassakānaṃ lokiyā.**  
*for those who have begun insight meditation, they are mundane.*

**Aniccasaññābhāvanānuyoganti ettha saññāsīsena vipassanā kathitā.**  
*"Aniccasaññābhāvanānuyogaṃ" (committed to developing the perception of impermanence): here, insight (vipassanā) is spoken of by way of its chief term, perception (saññā).*

**Yasmā panettha ānāpānakammaṭṭhānavasena abhiniviṭṭhāva bahū bhikkhū, tasmā sesakammaṭṭhānāni saṅkhepena kathetvā ānāpānakammaṭṭhānaṃ vitthārena kathento ānāpānassati, bhikhāvetiādimāha.**  
*Since many monks here had undertaken the practice of mindfulness of breathing as their meditation subject, the Teacher, after briefly stating the other meditation subjects, spoke of mindfulness of breathing in detail, saying "ānāpānassati, bhikkhave" and so forth.*

**Idaṃ pana ānāpānakammaṭṭhānaṃ sabbākārena visuddhimagge vitthāritaṃ, tasmā tattha vuttanayenevassa pāḷittho ca bhāvanānayo ca veditabbo.**  
*This meditation subject of mindfulness of breathing is explained in all aspects in the Visuddhimagga; therefore, its textual meaning and method of development should be understood in the way stated there.*

### §149

**149. Kāyaññatarantī pathavīkāyādīsu catūsu kāyesu aññataraṃ vadāmi, vāyo kāyaṃ vadāmīti attho.**  
*149. "Kāyaññataraṃ" (an aspect of the body) means: "I speak of one of the four bodies such as the earth-body, namely the wind-body (vāyo-kāya)."*

**Atha vā rūpāyatanaṃ…pe… kabaḷīkāro āhāroti pañcavīsati rūpakoṭṭhāsā rūpakāyo nāma.**  
*Alternatively, the form-body (rūpa-kāya) consists of twenty-five divisions of form, i.e., form-base ... pe ... nutritive food.*

**Tesu ānāpānaṃ phoṭṭhabbāyatane saṅgahitattā kāyaññataraṃ hoti, tasmāpi evamāha.**  
*Among these, the in-breaths and out-breaths are included in the tangible-base (phoṭṭhabbāyatana), thus it is "an aspect of the body"; for this reason also he said this.*

**Tasmātihāti yasmā catūsu kāyesu aññataraṃ vāyokāyaṃ, pañcavīsatirūpakoṭṭhāse vā rūpakāye aññataraṃ ānāpānaṃ anupassati, tasmā kāye kāyānupassīti attho.**  
*"Tasmātiha" (that is why): because he contemplates the in-breaths and out-breaths which are one among the four bodies, or one among the twenty-five divisions of form, therefore it is "contemplating the body in the body" (kāye kāyānupassī).*

**Evaṃ sabbattha attho veditabbo.**  
*In this way, the meaning should be understood everywhere.*

**Vedanāññatarantī tīsu vedanāsu aññataraṃ, sukhavedanaṃ sandhāyetaṃ vuttaṃ.**  
*"Vedanāññataraṃ" (an aspect of feelings) means one among the three feelings; this is said in reference to pleasant feeling (sukha-vedanā).*

**Sādhukaṃ manasikāranti pītipaṭisaṃveditādivasena uppannaṃ sundaramanasikāraṃ.**  
*"Sādhukaṃ manasikāraṃ" (careful application of mind) means the beautiful application of mind arising by way of experiencing rapture etc.*

**Kiṃ pana manasikāro sukhavedanā hotīti. Na hoti, desanāsīsaṃ panetaṃ.**  
*But is application of mind a pleasant feeling? No, it is not, but this is a teaching method using the chief term.*

**Yatheva hi ‘‘aniccasaññābhāvanānuyogamanuyuttā’’ti ettha saññānāmena paññā vuttā, evamidhāpi manasikāranāmena vedanā vuttāti veditabbā.**  
*Just as "perception of impermanence" speaks of wisdom by the name of perception, so here feeling is spoken of by the name of application of mind.*

**Etasmiṃ catukke paṭhamapade pītisīsena vedanā vuttā, dutiyapade sukhanti sarūpeneva vuttā.**  
*In the first step of this tetrad, feeling is spoken of by way of its chief term, rapture; in the second step, it is spoken of in its own form as pleasure.*

**Cittasaṅkhārapadadvaye ‘‘saññā ca vedanā ca cetasikā, ete dhammā cittapaṭibaddhā cittasaṅkhārā’’ti (paṭi. ma. 1.174) vacanato ‘‘vitakkavicāre ṭhapetvā sabbepi cittasampayuttakā dhammā cittasaṅkhāre saṅgahitā’’ti vacanato cittasaṅkhāranāmena vedanā vuttā.**  
*In the two steps on the mental process (citta-saṅkhāra), because of the passage: "Perception and feeling are mental, these states are bound up with the mind, they are mental processes" (Paṭisambhidāmagga 1.174), and because: "Excluding applied and sustained thought, all states associated with the mind are included in the mental process," feeling is spoken of by the name of mental process.*

**Taṃ sabbaṃ manasikāranāmena saṅgahetvā idha ‘‘sādhukaṃ manasikāra’’nti āha.**  
*Comprising all of that under the name of application of mind, he said here "sādhukaṃ manasikāraṃ."*

**Evaṃ santepi yasmā esā vedanā ārammaṇaṃ na hoti, tasmā vedanānupassanā na yujjatīti.**  
*Even if it is so, since this feeling is not the object (of the breath), how is the contemplation of feeling applicable?*

**No na yujjati, satipaṭṭhānavaṇṇanāyampi hi ‘‘taṃtaṃsukhādīnaṃ vatthuṃ ārammaṇaṃ katvā vedanāva vedayati, taṃ pana vedanāpavattiṃ upādāya ‘ahaṃ vedayāmī’ti vohāramattaṃ hotī’’ti vuttaṃ.**  
*No, it is indeed applicable; for in the explanation of the foundations of mindfulness it is said: "Making the base of that pleasure etc. the object, feeling itself feels, but depending on the occurrence of that feeling, it is merely a conventional expression to say 'I feel'."*

**Apica pītipaṭisaṃvedītiādīnaṃ atthavaṇṇanāyametassa parihāro vuttoyeva.**  
*Furthermore, the explanation of the meaning of "experiencing rapture" etc. gives the resolution of this point.*

**Vuttañhetaṃ visuddhimagge –**  
*Indeed, this is stated in the Visuddhimagga:*

**‘‘Dvīhākārehi pīti paṭisaṃviditā hoti ārammaṇato ca asammohato ca.**  
*"Rapture is experienced in two ways: by way of the object and by way of non-delusion.*

**Kathaṃ ārammaṇato pīti paṭisaṃviditā hoti?**  
*How is rapture experienced by way of the object?*

**Sappītike dve jhāne samāpajjati, tassa samāpattikkhaṇe jhānapaṭilābhena ārammaṇato pīti paṭisaṃviditā hoti ārammaṇassa paṭisaṃviditattā.**  
*One enters the two jhānas accompanied by rapture; at the moment of attainment, by the acquisition of the jhāna, rapture is experienced by way of the object, because the object is experienced.*

**Kathaṃ asammohato (pīti paṭisaṃviditā hoti)?**  
*How is rapture experienced by way of non-delusion?*

**Sappītike dve jhāne samāpajjitvā vuṭṭhāya jhānasampayuttaṃ pītiṃ khayato vayato sammasati, tassa vipassanākkhaṇe lakkhaṇapaṭivedhā asammohato pīti paṭisaṃviditā hoti.**  
*Having entered the two jhānas accompanied by rapture and emerged from them, one contemplates the rapture associated with the jhāna as impermanent and passing away; at the moment of insight, by the penetration of its characteristics, rapture is experienced by way of non-delusion.*

**Vuttampi cetaṃ paṭisambhidāyaṃ ‘dīghaṃ assāsavasena cittassa ekaggataṃ avikkhepaṃ pajānato sati upaṭṭhitā hoti, tāya satiyā, tena ñāṇena sā pīti paṭisaṃviditā hotī’ti.**  
*It is also stated in the Paṭisambhidāmagga: 'For one who knows the one-pointedness of mind and non-distraction by way of a long in-breath, mindfulness is established; by that mindfulness, by that knowledge, that rapture is experienced.'*

**Eteneva nayena avasesapadānipi atthato veditabbānī’’ti.**  
*In this same way, the remaining steps should be understood according to their meaning."*

**Iti yatheva jhānapaṭilābhena ārammaṇato pītisukhacittasaṅkhārā paṭisaṃviditā honti, evaṃ imināpi jhānasampayuttena vedanāsaṅkhātamanasikārapaṭilābhena ārammaṇato vedanā paṭisaṃviditā hoti.**  
*Thus, just as by the acquisition of jhāna, rapture, pleasure, and mental processes are experienced by way of the object, so by the acquisition of the application of mind known as feeling associated with that jhāna, feeling is experienced by way of the object.*

**Tasmā suvuttametaṃ hoti ‘‘vedanāsu vedanānupassī tasmiṃ samaye bhikkhu viharatī’’ti.**  
*Therefore, it is well said: "At that time a mendicant is meditating by observing an aspect of feelings."*

**Nāhaṃ, bhikkhave, muṭṭhassatissa asampajānassā ti ettha ayamadhippāyo –**  
*"Nāhaṁ, bhikkhave, muṭṭhassatissa asampajānassa" (There is no development of mindfulness of breathing for someone who is unmindful and lacks awareness, I say): the intention here is this—*

**yasmā cittapaṭisaṃvedī assasissāmītiādinā nayena pavatto bhikkhu kiñcāpi assāsapassāsanimittaṃ ārammaṇaṃ karoti, tassa pana cittassa ārammaṇe satiñca sampajaññañca upaṭṭhapetvā pavattanato citte cittānupassīyeva nāmesa hoti.**  
*since a monk proceeding in the way of "breathing in experiencing the mind" etc. indeed makes the sign of the in-and-out breaths his object, but because his mind arises having established mindfulness and clear comprehension on that object, he is one who is "contemplating the mind in the mind."*

**Na hi muṭṭhassatissa asampajānassa ānāpānassatibhāvanā atthi.**  
*Indeed, there is no development of mindfulness of breathing for one who is unmindful and lacks awareness.*

**Tasmā ārammaṇato cittapaṭisaṃviditādivasena citte cittānupassī tasmiṃ samaye bhikkhu viharatīti.**  
*Therefore, by experiencing the mind by way of the object etc., "at that time a mendicant is meditating by observing an aspect of the mind."*

**So yaṁ taṃ abhijjhādomanassānaṃ pahānaṃ, taṃ paññāya disvā sādhukaṃ ajjhupekkhitā hotī ti ettha abhijjhāya kāmacchandanīvaraṇaṃ, domanassavasena byāpādanīvaraṇaṃ dassitaṃ.**  
*"So yaṁ taṃ abhijjhādomanassānaṃ pahānaṃ, taṃ paññāya disvā sādhukaṃ ajjhupekkhitā hoti" (Having seen with wisdom the giving up of covetousness and displeasure, they watch over closely with equanimity): here, by covetousness (abhijjhā), the hindrance of sensual desire is shown, and by displeasure (domanassa), the hindrance of ill-will is shown.*

**Idañhi catukkaṃ vipassanāvaseneva vuttaṃ, dhammānupassanā ca nīvaraṇapabbādivasena chabbidhā hoti, tassā nīvaraṇapabbaṃ ādi, tassapi idaṃ nīvaraṇadvayaṃ ādi, iti dhammānupassanāya ādiṃ dassetuṃ ‘‘abhijjhādomanassāna’’nti āha.**  
*This tetrad is spoken of solely by way of insight (vipassanā). Contemplation of principles (dhammānupassanā) is sixfold by way of the section on hindrances etc.; the section on hindrances is the first of these, and these two hindrances are the first of those; thus, to show the beginning of the contemplation of principles, he said "abhijjhādomanassānaṃ."*

**Pahānanti aniccānupassanāya niccasaññaṃ pajahatīti evaṃ pahānakarañāṇaṃ adhippetaṃ.**  
*"Pahānaṃ" (giving up) means the knowledge that gives up, as in "by the contemplation of impermanence one gives up the perception of permanence."*

**Taṃ paññāya disvāti taṃ aniccānupassanā-virāgānupassanā-nirodhānupassanā-paṭinissaggānupassanāñāṇasaṅkhātaṃ pahānañāṇaṃ aparāya vipassanāpaññāya, tampi aparāyāti evaṃ vipassanāparamparaṃ dasseti.**  
*"Taṃ paññāya disvā" (having seen it with wisdom): having seen that knowledge of giving up—which consists of the knowledge of the contemplation of impermanence, contemplation of fading away, contemplation of cessation, and contemplation of letting go—with a subsequent insight wisdom, and that with another, thus showing the succession of insight.*

**Ajjhupekkhitā hotīti yañca samathapaṭipannaṃ ajjhupekkhati, yañca ekato upaṭṭhānaṃ ajjhupekkhatīti dvidhā ajjhupekkhati nāma.**  
*"Ajjhupekkhitā hoti" (watches over closely with equanimity): one watches over with equanimity in two ways—watching over the mind that has entered calm, and watching over the unified establishment of mindfulness.*

**Tatthā sahajātānampi ajjhupekkhanā hoti ārammaṇasapipi ajjhupekkhanā, idha ārammaṇaajjhupekkhanā adhippetā.**  
*Therein, there is equanimity toward co-nascent states and equanimity toward the object; here, equanimity toward the object is intended.*

**Tasmātihā, bhikhāveti yasmā aniccānupassī assasissāmītiādinā nayena pavatto na kevalaṃ nīvaraṇādidhamme, abhijjhādomanassasīsena pana vuttānaṃ dhammānaṃ pahānañāṇampi paññāya disvā ajjhupekkhitā hoti, tasmā ‘‘dhammesu dhammānupassī tasmiṃ samaye bhikkhu viharatī’’ti veditabbo.**  
*"Tasmātiha, bhikkhave" (that is why, monks): since one proceeding in the way of "breathing in observing impermanence" etc. sees with wisdom not only the states of hindrances etc., but also the knowledge of giving up those states mentioned under the heading of covetousness and displeasure, and watches over them with equanimity, therefore it should be understood: "At that time a mendicant is meditating by observing an aspect of principles."*

### §150

**150. Pavicinatīti aniccādivasena pavicinati.**  
*150. "Pavicinati" (investigates) means investigates by way of impermanence etc.*

**Itaraṃ padadvayaṃ etasseva vevacanaṃ.**  
*The other two words ("explores" and "inquires") are synonyms of this.*

**Nirāmisāti nikkilesā.**  
*"Nirāmisā" (not of the flesh) means free of defilements.*

**Passambhatīti kāyikacetasikadarathapaṭippassaddhiyā kāyopi cittampi passambhati.**  
*"Passambhati" (becomes tranquil) means both the body and mind become tranquil by the calming of bodily and mental distress.*

**Samādhiyatīti sammā ṭhapiyati, appanāpattaṃ viya hoti.**  
*"Samādhiyati" (becomes immersed in samādhi) means becomes well-established, as if reaching absorption (appanā).*

**Ajjhupekkhitā hotīti sahajātaajjhupekkhanāya ajjhupekkhitā hoti.**  
*"Ajjhupekkhitā hoti" (watches over closely with equanimity) means one watches over by way of equanimity toward co-nascent states.*

**Evaṃ cuddasavidhena kāyapariggāhakassa bhikkhuno tasmiṃ kāye sati satisambojjhaṅgo, satiyā sampayuttaṃ ñāṇaṃ dhammavicayasambojjhaṅgo, taṃsampayuttameva kāyikacetasikavīriyaṃ vīriyasambojjhaṅgo, pīti, passaddhi, cittekaggatā samādhisambojjhaṅgo, imesaṃ channaṃ sambojjhaṅgānaṃ anosakkanaanativattanasaṅkhāto majjhattākāro upekkhāsambojjhaṅgo.**  
*Thus, for the monk who grasps the body in fourteen ways, the mindfulness in that body is the awakening factor of mindfulness; the wisdom associated with that mindfulness is the awakening factor of investigation of principles; the bodily and mental energy associated with that is the awakening factor of energy; [and similarly] rapture, tranquility, one-pointedness of mind is the awakening factor of immersion; the neutral state consisting of the non-slacking and non-overrunning of these six awakening factors is the awakening factor of equanimity.*

**Yatheva hi samappavattesu assesu sārathino ‘‘ayaṃ olīyatī’’ti tudanaṃ vā, ‘‘ayaṃ atidhāvatī’’ti ākaḍḍhanaṃ vā natthi, kevalaṃ evaṃ passamānassa ṭhitākārova hoti, evameva imesaṃ channaṃ sambojjhaṅgānaṃ anosakkanaanativattanasaṅkhāto majjhattākāro upekkhāsambojjhaṅgo nāma hoti.**  
*Just as when horses are running evenly, there is no whipping by the charioteer saying "this one is lagging," nor pulling back saying "this one is running too fast," but he merely looks on while remaining still; in the same way, the neutral state consisting of the non-slacking and non-overrunning of these six awakening factors is called the awakening factor of equanimity.*

**Ettāvatā kiṃ kathitaṃ? Ekacittakkhaṇikā nānārasalakkhaṇā vipassanāsambojjhaṅgā nāma kathitā.**  
*What is declared by this much? The factors of awakening of insight, which exist in a single mind-moment and have different functions and characteristics, are declared.*

### §152

**152. Vivekanissantintiādīni vuttatthāneva.**  
*152. "Vivekanissitaṃ" (rely on seclusion) etc. have the meanings already stated.*

**Ettha pana ānāpānapariggāhikā sati lokiyā hoti, lokiyā ānāpānā lokiyasatipaṭṭhānaṃ paripūrenti, lokiyā satipaṭṭhānā lokuttarabojjhaṅge paripūrenti, lokuttarā bojjhaṅgā vijjāvimuttiphalanibbānaṃ paripūrenti.**  
*Here, however, the mindfulness that grasps the breathing is mundane; the mundane mindfulness of breathing fulfills the mundane foundations of mindfulness; the mundane foundations of mindfulness fulfill the supramundane awakening factors; the supramundane awakening factors fulfill knowledge and freedom, which is the fruit and Nibbāna.*

**Iti lokiyassa āgataṭṭhāne lokiyaṃ kathitaṃ, lokuttarassa āgataṭṭhāne lokuttaraṃ kathitanti.**  
*Thus, where the mundane occurs, the mundane is spoken of, and where the supramundane occurs, the supramundane is spoken of.*

**Thero panāha ‘‘aññattha evaṃ hoti, imasmiṃ pana sutte lokuttaraṃ upari āgataṃ, lokiyā ānāpānā lokiyasatipaṭṭhāne paripūrenti, lokiyā satipaṭṭhānā lokiye bojjhaṅge paripūrenti, lokiyā bojjhaṅgā lokuttaraṃ vijjāvimuttiphalanibbānaṃ paripūrenti, vijjāvimuttipadena hi idha vijjāvimuttiphalanibbānaṃ ajjhapeta’’nti.**  
*But the Elder says: "Elsewhere it is so, but in this sutta the supramundane occurs above: the mundane mindfulness of breathing fulfills the mundane foundations of mindfulness; the mundane foundations of mindfulness fulfill the mundane factors of awakening; the mundane factors of awakening fulfill supramundane knowledge and freedom, which is the fruit and Nibbāna, for by the word 'vijjāvimutti' (knowledge and freedom) here, the fruit and Nibbāna of knowledge and freedom is intended."*
