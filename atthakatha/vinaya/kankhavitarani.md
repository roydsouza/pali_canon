# Kaṅkhāvitaraṇī (The Dispeller of Doubts)

**Navigation**: [[INDEX|Pali Canon Vault]] / [[atthakatha/INDEX|Atthakathā]] / [[atthakatha/vinaya/INDEX|Vinaya Atthakathā]]

*Written by Ācariya Buddhaghosa (5th century CE).*

---

## Overview

The **Kaṅkhāvitaraṇī** is the classical commentary (*atthakathā*) on the Bhikkhu and Bhikkhuni Pātimokkhas (the monastic codes). It serves as a practical, concise guide for monastics, explaining the exact definition of terms, the boundaries of the rules, and the precise conditions under which an offense (*āpatti*) is committed or avoided.

Unlike the larger **Samantapāsādikā** (which comments on the entire Vinaya Piṭaka), the Kaṅkhāvitaraṇī focuses exclusively on the Pātimokkha rules, making it the primary manual for daily monastic discipline.

---

## Structure & Commentary Divisions

The text is divided into two main parts, corresponding to the two codes:

### 1. Bhikkhupātimokkha-vaṇṇanā (Commentary on the Monks' Code)
Analyzes the 227 rules of the Bhikkhu Pātimokkha (see [[mula/vinaya/patimokkha_bhikkhu|Bhikkhu Pātimokkha]]):
*   **Pārājika-vaṇṇanā**: Explains the 4 rules of defeat (sexual intercourse, theft, killing a human, false claims of spiritual attainments).
*   **Saṅghādisesa-vaṇṇanā**: Explains the 13 rules requiring community meetings.
*   **Aniyata-vaṇṇanā**: Explains the 2 indefinite cases.
*   **Nissaggiya Pācittiya-vaṇṇanā**: Explains the 30 rules involving forfeiture and confession.
*   **Pācittiya-vaṇṇanā**: Explains the 92 rules requiring confession.
*   **Pāṭidesanīya-vaṇṇanā**: Explains the 4 rules to be acknowledged.
*   **Sekhiya-vaṇṇanā**: Explains the 75 rules of training (etiquette and behavior).
*   **Adhikaraṇasamatha-vaṇṇanā**: Explains the 7 rules for settling litigation.

### 2. Bhikkhunīpātimokkha-vaṇṇanā (Commentary on the Nuns' Code)
Analyzes the 311 rules of the Bhikkhunī Pātimokkha (see [[mula/vinaya/patimokkha_bhikkhuni|Bhikkhunī Pātimokkha]]):
*   Focuses on the rules unique to nuns (*asādhāraṇa-sikkhāpada*) and references the monks' commentary for shared rules (*sādhāraṇa-sikkhāpada*).

---

## Doctrinal Connection: The Precepts

In the Theravāda tradition, monastic discipline is built upon and expands the foundational training in virtue (*sīla*) outlined in lay precepts:
*   The **[[matika/five_precepts|Five Precepts]]** are fully incorporated and expanded:
    *   *Abstaining from killing* is expanded into the 3rd Pārājika (murder) and Pacittiya 61 (killing animals).
    *   *Abstaining from stealing* is expanded into the 2nd Pārājika.
    *   *Abstaining from sexual misconduct* is heightened to absolute celibacy (1st Pārājika).
    *   *Abstaining from false speech* is expanded into the 4th Pārājika (lying about attainments) and Pacittiya 1–2 (deliberate lying and slander).
    *   *Abstaining from intoxicants* is expanded into Pacittiya 51.
*   The **[[matika/eight_precepts|Eight Precepts]]** (practiced by lay devotees on Uposatha days) mimic monastic life and find their source in the Pātimokkha rules regarding afternoon eating (Pacittiya 37), entertainment (Pacittiya 85), high beds (Pacittiya 84), and money handling (Nissaggiya Pacittiya 18).

---

## Commentary Highlights

*   **Definition of Intent (*Cetanā*)**: Buddhaghosa repeatedly emphasizes that the presence or absence of intent determines whether a rule has been violated.
*   **Mitigating Circumstances (*Anāpatti*)**: The text defines the status of "no offense" for those who are insane (*ummattaka*), in extreme pain (*khittacitta*), or the first offender whose actions led to the rule's formulation (*ādikammika*).
