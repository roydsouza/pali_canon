# Ānanda Thera

**Navigation**: [[INDEX|Pali Canon Vault]] / [[people/INDEX|People]]

**Ānanda** was the Buddha's cousin and his personal attendant (*upatthāka*) for the last twenty-five years of the Buddha's life. He was declared foremost in five categories: great learning (*bahu-sutta*), mindfulness (*sati*), behavior (*gati*), resoluteness (*dhitī*), and ministering (*upatthāka*).

---

## Biography & Legacy

*   **Role**: He memorized all the Buddha's discourses. At the First Buddhist Council (held in [[places/rajagaha|Rājagaha]] shortly after the Buddha's Parinibbāna), Ānanda recited all the discourses (*Sutta Piṭaka*), beginning each with the famous phrase *"Evaṃ me sutaṃ"* ("Thus have I heard").
*   **Establishment of Bhikkhunī Sangha**: Under the urging of Mahāpajāpatī Gotamī, Ānanda interceded with the Buddha, obtaining permission for women to ordain as nuns (*bhikkhunīs*).
*   **Awakening**: He attained Arhathood on the very night before the First Council began, while in the act of lying down to sleep (not in any of the four standard postures).

---

## Associated Places

*   [[places/savatthi|Sāvatthī]] — Residence in Jetavana where he heard most suttas.
*   [[places/kosambi|Kosambī]] — Where he taught several suttas (such as to the brahmin Uṇṇābha).
*   [[places/vesali|Vesālī]] — The place where the Buddha passed away nearby and where Ānanda spent much time.

---

## Key Discourses (Suttas)

*   **[[mula/sutta/digha_nikaya/dn15|DN 15: Mahānidānasutta]]** — The Great Discourse on Causation. It begins with Ānanda saying that Dependent Origination seems simple, and the Buddha rebukes him, warning that it is deep and difficult to see.
*   **[[mula/sutta/samyutta_nikaya/INDEX|SN 51.15: Unṇābhabrāhmaṇasutta]]** — Ānanda explains to the brahmin Uṇṇābha how desire (*chanda*) is used to end desire.
*   **[[mula/sutta/khuddaka_nikaya/theragatha/INDEX|THAG 17.3]]** — Verses of Ānanda in the Theragāthā.

---

## Doctrinal Highlights

*   **Thus Have I Heard**: The phrase denotes Ānanda's direct auditory witness.
*   **Desire Ending Desire**: In SN 51.15, he uses the simile of walking to a park: one needs the desire to reach the park, but once there, that specific desire is stilled.
