# Right Concentration (Sammāsamādhi)

**Navigation**: [[../INDEX|Pali Canon Vault]] / [[INDEX|Mātika]] / [[noble_eightfold_path|Noble Eightfold Path]]

---

## Canonical Definition

> "Katamo ca, bhikkhave, sammāsamādhi? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu vivicceva kāmehi vivicca akusalehi dhammehi savitakkaṃ savicāraṃ vivekajaṃ pītisukhaṃ paṭhamaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati; 
> vitakkavicārānaṃ vūpasamā ajjhattaṃ sampasādanaṃ cetaso ekodibhāvaṃ avitakkaṃ avicāraṃ samādhijaṃ pītisukhaṃ dutiyaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati; 
> pītiyā ca virāgā upekkhako ca viharati sato ca sampajāno sukhañca kāyena paṭisaṃvedeti yaṃ taṃ ariyā ācikkhanti: ‘upekkhako satimā sukhavihārī’ti tatiyaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati; 
> sukhassa ca pahānā dukkhassa ca pahānā pubbeva somanassadomanassānaṃ atthaṅgamā adukkhamasukhaṃ upekkhāsatipārisuddhiṃ catutthaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati—ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, sammāsamādhi."
> 
> *“And what, bhikkhus, is right concentration? Here, secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unwholesome states, a bhikkhu enters and dwells in the first jhāna, which is accompanied by directed thought and evaluation, with rapture and pleasure born of seclusion.
> 
> With the stilling of directed thought and evaluation, he enters and dwells in the second jhāna, which has internal confidence and unification of mind, is free from directed thought and evaluation, and has rapture and pleasure born of concentration.
> 
> With the fading away as well of rapture, he dwells equanimous, mindful, and clearly comprehending, and experiences pleasure with the body; he enters and dwells in the third jhāna, of which the noble ones declare: ‘He is equanimous, mindful, one who dwells pleasantly.’
> 
> With the abandoning of pleasure and pain, and with the previous disappearance of joy and grief, he enters and dwells in the fourth jhāna, which has neither-pain-nor-pleasure and purity of mindfulness due to equanimity:
> this, bhikkhus, is called right concentration.”*
> — **SN 45.8 (Vibhaṅgasutta)**

## Detailed Description

**Right Concentration (*Sammāsamādhi*)** is the unification and stabilization of the mind (*cittass'ekaggatā*). In the Pali Canon, Right Concentration is specifically defined as the four **Jhānas** (deep meditative absorptions). It is the culmination of the mental training group (*samādhi*), transforming the mind into a sharp, bright, and immovable instrument capable of penetrating reality and realizing wisdom.

Right Concentration is developed through four progressive stages of absorption:
1.  **First Jhāna**: Secluded from sensual desires and unwholesome states, the mind experiences directed thought (*vitakka*), evaluation (*vicāra*), rapture (*pīti*), and pleasure (*sukha*) born of physical and mental seclusion.
2.  **Second Jhāna**: As thinking and evaluation fall silent, the mind becomes unified and confident, experiencing rapture and pleasure born of concentration (*samādhijaṃ*).
3.  **Third Jhāna**: As rapture (exuberant joy) fades, the meditator remains equanimous, mindful, and clearly comprehending, experiencing a refined, bodily pleasure (*sukha*).
4.  **Fourth Jhāna**: Relinquishing both pleasure and pain, the mind enters a state of deep, silent stillness, characterized by absolute equanimity and purity of mindfulness (*upekkhāsatipārisuddhi*).

Right Concentration is not a trance of self-hypnosis or blankness. In the fourth jhāna, the mind is described as "bright, pure, unblemished, malleable, wieldy, steady, and attained to imperturbability." In this state, it is directed toward the three knowledges: recollection of past lives, the passing away and rebirth of beings according to their kamma, and the direct realization of the ending of mental defilements (*āsavakkhaya*).

## Sutta References

The following suttas in the vault mention, describe, or analyze Right Concentration:

*   [[../mula/sutta/majjhima_nikaya/mn111|MN 111: Anupadasutta]] — Ven. Sāriputta's progressive mastery, demonstrating how he investigated each mental factor one-by-one in sequence through the four jhānas and formless attainments.
*   [[../mula/sutta/digha_nikaya/dn2|DN 2: Sāmaññaphalasutta]] — Provides a detailed description of the four jhānas, illustrating them with beautiful similes (such as bath powder, a spring-fed lake, lotuses in water, and a man wrapped in a white cloth) and linking them to insight.
*   [[../mula/sutta/anguttara_nikaya/an5_28|AN 5.28: Pañcaṅgikasutta]] — Focuses on right concentration with five factors, describing how the qualities of rapture, pleasure, and bright awareness saturate the physical body.
*   [[../mula/sutta/anguttara_nikaya/an9_36|AN 9.36: Jhānasutta]] — Analyzes each jhāna and formless attainment as a springboard for insight, showing how one treats the absorption as impermanent and unsatisfactory to achieve release.
*   [[../mula/sutta/anguttara_nikaya/an4_41|AN 4.41: Samādhibhāvanāsutta]] — Explains the four ways of developing concentration: for pleasant abiding in this life (jhānas), for gaining knowledge and vision (divine eye), for mindfulness and clear comprehension, and for the ending of defilements.
*   [[../mula/sutta/samyutta_nikaya/INDEX|SN 45.8: Vibhaṅgasutta]] — The primary path analysis defining each of the eight path factors.
