# Right Mindfulness (Sammāsati)

**Navigation**: [[../INDEX|Pali Canon Vault]] / [[INDEX|Mātika]] / [[noble_eightfold_path|Noble Eightfold Path]]

---

## Canonical Definition

> "Katamā ca, bhikkhave, sammāsati? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu kāye kāyānupassī viharati ātāpī sampajāno satimā, vineyya loke abhijjhādomanassaṃ; 
> vedanāsu vedanānupassī viharati ātāpī sampajāno satimā, vineyya loke abhijjhādomanassaṃ; 
> citte cittānupassī viharati ātāpī sampajāno satimā, vineyya loke abhijjhādomanassaṃ; 
> dhammesu dhammānupassī viharati ātāpī sampajāno satimā, vineyya loke abhijjhādomanassaṃ—ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, sammāsati."
> 
> *“And what, bhikkhus, is right mindfulness? Here, a bhikkhu dwells contemplating the body in the body, ardent, clearly comprehending, and mindful, putting away covetousness and displeasure concerning the world; 
> he dwells contemplating feelings in feelings, ardent, clearly comprehending, and mindful, putting away covetousness and displeasure concerning the world; 
> he dwells contemplating mind in mind, ardent, clearly comprehending, and mindful, putting away covetousness and displeasure concerning the world; 
> he dwells contemplating phenomena in phenomena, ardent, clearly comprehending, and mindful, putting away covetousness and displeasure concerning the world:
> this, bhikkhus, is called right mindfulness.”*
> — **SN 45.8 (Vibhaṅgasutta)**

## Detailed Description

**Right Mindfulness (*Sammāsati*)** is the quality of active, non-reactive awareness. The Pali word *Sati* originally means "memory" or "recollection," and in the context of meditation, it refers to keeping the mind anchored in the present moment, remembering to monitor and observe physical and mental processes with clarity and without judgment.

Right Mindfulness is developed through the **Four Foundations of Mindfulness** (*cattāro satipaṭṭhānā*):
1.  **Contemplation of the Body (*Kāyānupassanā*)**: Observing the physical body, including breathing (*ānāpāna*), postures, elements, bodily parts, and the decay of the corpse. It anchors the mind in physical reality.
2.  **Contemplation of Feelings (*Vedanānupassanā*)**: Observing the hedonic quality of experience (pleasant, unpleasant, or neutral sensations) as they arise and pass away, preventing them from automatically triggering craving or aversion.
3.  **Contemplation of the Mind (*Cittānupassanā*)**: Observing the current state of consciousness (e.g., whether it is with or without lust, anger, delusion, concentration, or distraction).
4.  **Contemplation of Phenomena (*Dhammānupassanā*)**: Contemplating mental factors and categories in terms of their arising and ceasing, such as the Five Hindrances, the Five Aggregates, the Six Sense Bases, the Seven Awakening Factors, and the Four Noble Truths.

Right Mindfulness operates with three auxiliary qualities: **ardency** (*ātāpī* — indicating energy and effort), **clear comprehension** (*sampajāna* — indicating wisdom and situational awareness), and **mindfulness** (*satimā* — indicating recollection). It gathers and stabilizes the mind, serving as the immediate basis for Right Concentration.
